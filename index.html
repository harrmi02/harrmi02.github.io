Love & Information

Fall 2015
Stage Manager
Luther College Visual & Performing Arts

Over the course of 90 minutes, Caryl Churchill’s Love and Information offers the audience more than 50 brief glimpses of the human experience. The unique challenge of this show, beyond the versatility required of the actors, is the ingenuity and responsiveness required of everyone involved in the production. Churchill’s script contains only dialogue, scene titles, and a few briefs notes. The given circumstances, from setting to subtext to scene order, are left entirely up to the creative team and cast. Due to the wide variation in settings, we opted for a simple, slightly abstract set that could easily suggest a wide variety of locations. We relied on lighting, projections, sound cues, costumes, and the actors to provide the specific context of each scene.

As the stage manager, I acted as the memory of the show and ensured clear and consistent communication between the various people involved in the show. During the rehearsal process, I spent much of my time maintaining a massive spreadsheet that was shared between the members of the production staff. We used the spreadsheet to keep track of any decisions that the director and cast made regarding the setting and characters of each scene. The designers then used it to track the various technical elements that each scene would need. Additionally, I sent out rehearsal reports to the cast and production staff after each rehearsal explaining what the cast had worked on and detailing any new decisions or changes that had come up. In this way, I kept the production staff up to date and tracked each decision in case we needed to refer back later on. Outside of rehearsal, I assisted in building the set by assembling and connecting the platforms that made up the structure of the stage, painting the set, and assisting in the placement of the vertical poles. During the run of the show, I was responsible for calling the light, sound, and projection cues to smoothly transition between each scene.

Metamorphoses

Spring 2015
Assistant Stage Manager
Luther College Visual & Performing Arts

This play, adapted from Ovid’s poem by Mary Zimmerman, guides the audience through a series of mythical tales thematically centered on transformation and literally centered on and in a pool of water. The primary technical challenge of the show was, of course, the use of water and all that it entails. Our pool was four feet deep in the center with three shallower levels on each side. It was surrounded by two feet of platform on all sides, except for the ramp in the center of the upstage side which was flanked by two elaborate archways. The ramp covered an underwater entrance for the actors, and the archways were decorated to look like they were mid-transformation.

As one of two assistant stage managers, my primary focus was on preparing the understudies to go on for any role. At the beginning of each rehearsal, I led warmups for the full cast. During the first few weeks of rehearsal, I recorded the blocking of each scene as it was established in order to act as a reference so that the understudies could check the accuracy of their own notes. I had regular discussions with the understudies to discuss their thoughts and questions and to help them set goals for memorization. As the performances drew closer and the blocking became consistent, I used the rehearsal time to coach the understudies separately on the scenes they were most likely to perform. In addition, I assisted in the construction of the set, including building and lining the pool, covering the seams with muslin and paint, painting the set, and using papier mâ ché to create the stone on the archways. During performances, I facilitated transitions and acted as a point of contact between the cast and the stage manager for cues, as well as making a brief cameo appearance in the show.
